# Sideline Recognition: When to Remove an Athlete

By **Dr. Neil J. Patel, MD, MBA**Sports Neurology & Brain Injury MedicineLast reviewed: May 26, 2026

**Educational content — not medical advice.**

This article is for general educational purposes only and does not establish a physician–patient relationship, diagnose any condition, or replace evaluation by a qualified clinician. The most important moment in concussion management is also the simplest: the decision to remove an athlete from play after a possible head impact. When in doubt, sit them out.

### Observable signs that warrant immediate removal

* Loss of consciousness, even briefly
* Slow to get up after a head or body impact
* Disorientation, confusion, or inability to respond to questions appropriately
* Vacant or blank stare
* Balance problems, unsteadiness, or stumbling
* Forgetting events before or after the impact
* Slurred or incoherent speech
* Visible facial injury combined with any of the above

### Symptoms reported by the athlete that warrant removal

* Headache or "pressure" in the head
* Nausea or vomiting
* Dizziness or balance problems
* Sensitivity to light or noise
* Feeling foggy, slowed, or "not right"
* Vision changes

If any of these are present after a possible head impact, the athlete must be removed from play and may not return that day, even if symptoms resolve quickly. This is the rule from the [CDC HEADS UP program for coaches](https://www.cdc.gov/heads-up/response/index.html) and from every major sports medicine organization.

### Tools that support — but do not replace — clinical judgment

The [Sport Concussion Assessment Tool, 6th edition (SCAT6)](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/57/11/622) is the standardized sideline assessment for athletes 13 and older. The Child SCAT6 covers ages 8-12. These tools structure the evaluation but do not diagnose concussion on their own — concussion remains a clinical diagnosis.

### The non-negotiable rule

A symptomatic athlete is not returning to play. There is no upside to forcing the decision, and the downside includes Second Impact Syndrome, prolonged recovery, and long-term risk. An athlete recovering from a suspected concussion should be evaluated by a clinician trained in concussion management before beginning a graduated return-to-play protocol.
